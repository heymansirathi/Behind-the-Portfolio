# Good Analytics Begins Before the Dashboard

> *The first question I ask is rarely, “Which chart should I use?”*
>
> *It is usually, “What decision are we trying to make?”*

---

A few years ago, I thought building the dashboard was the difficult part.

Choosing the visual.

Writing the SQL.

Organizing the layout.

Making everything fit.

I was wrong.

The most important work usually happens before any of that begins.

It happens during the conversations where someone says:

> “We need a dashboard.”

That sentence sounds specific, but it often is not.

A dashboard for whom?

To answer what?

To support which decision?

To solve what problem?

Those questions shape everything that comes next.

---

## The Same Data Can Mean Different Things

Two people can ask for the same report while needing completely different answers.

An executive may want to understand whether the organization is growing.

A branch manager may want to know why one location is underperforming.

A lender may be focused on risk.

An operations team may need to identify where a process is slowing down.

The data may be similar.

The decision is not.

That is why I try not to begin with the request itself.

I begin with the person behind it.

---

## Questions Before Tools

Before opening Power BI, IBM Cognos, SQL, or Excel, I usually ask:

- Who will use this?
- What decision should it support?
- What action might follow?
- What question is difficult to answer today?
- What information is already available?
- What would make this report genuinely useful?
- What does not belong here?

These questions often reveal that the original request is only part of the real need.

Sometimes someone asks for a chart when what they actually need is an alert.

Sometimes they ask for ten KPIs when only three influence the decision.

Sometimes they ask for a new report when the existing one simply needs to be redesigned.

---

## I Still Use a Notebook

Even though most of my work is digital, I still keep a notebook beside my laptop.

I write down questions.

I sketch layouts.

I draw arrows between ideas.

I list what the user needs to know first, second, and third.

It helps me think before I build.

There is something useful about slowing down long enough to understand the problem without being distracted by the tool.

---

## Requirements Are Not Just Fields

Good requirements are not simply a list of columns.

They include context.

Why does this metric matter?

How frequently should it change?

What should happen when it moves?

Who is responsible for acting?

What comparison gives the number meaning?

A metric without context is only a value.

A metric connected to a decision becomes insight.

---

## The Dashboard Comes Later

Once the business question is clear, the technical work becomes much easier.

The right data becomes easier to identify.

The right KPIs become more obvious.

The visual hierarchy becomes clearer.

The dashboard stops feeling like a collection of charts and starts feeling like a guided answer.

That is the difference between reporting and decision support.

---

## What I Have Learned

Good analytics does not begin with visualization.

It begins with listening.

It begins with understanding the audience.

It begins with being curious enough to ask why the report is needed in the first place.

The dashboard is important.

But it is not the beginning.

> **A dashboard is only as useful as the questions that existed before it.**
