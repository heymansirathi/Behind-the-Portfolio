# The Questions I Ask Before Building Anything

> *Before I build, I ask.*
>
> *Before I design, I listen.*

---

I ask a lot of questions.

My teammates would probably confirm that.

Not because I want to slow the process down.

Because I have learned that a few thoughtful questions at the beginning can prevent weeks of confusion later.

These are the questions I return to most often.

---

## Who Is This Really For?

Not just the department.

Not just the title.

The actual person.

What do they already know?

What are they responsible for?

How much time do they have?

What decisions are they expected to make?

A report designed for an executive should not look like one designed for an analyst.

A dashboard designed for daily operations should not behave like a monthly scorecard.

Understanding the user changes the solution.

---

## What Decision Should This Support?

Every useful product should connect to a decision.

Should the user investigate?

Approve?

Compare?

Prioritize?

Escalate?

Plan?

If I cannot explain what decision the work supports, I probably do not understand the request well enough yet.

---

## What Problem Are We Actually Solving?

Sometimes the requested solution is not the real solution.

Someone may ask for a new dashboard because the existing process is slow.

But perhaps the real issue is missing data.

Or unclear ownership.

Or inconsistent definitions.

Or too many manual steps.

I try to separate the requested output from the underlying problem.

---

## What Happens Today?

Before changing a process, I want to understand it.

Where does the information come from?

Who prepares it?

How long does it take?

What work is repeated?

Where do errors happen?

What frustrates the user?

The current process usually contains valuable clues.

---

## What Would Success Look Like?

Would success mean:

- Less manual work?
- Faster decisions?
- Fewer questions?
- Better consistency?
- Improved visibility?
- Earlier identification of risk?
- More confidence in the numbers?

A clear definition of success makes it easier to evaluate whether the solution worked.

---

## What Does Not Belong Here?

This question may be just as important as asking what to include.

Not every metric deserves space.

Not every filter adds value.

Not every stakeholder request belongs on the same page.

Removing unnecessary information often improves the experience more than adding another visual.

---

## Would Someone Understand This Without Me?

I ask this before considering the work finished.

Would the title make sense?

Are the definitions clear?

Is the comparison obvious?

Can someone identify what matters?

Would they know what to do next?

The goal is not to create dependence on the person who built the report.

The goal is to create clarity.

---

## What Could Go Wrong?

This is especially important with data and AI.

What if the source is delayed?

What if a field is missing?

What if the model is wrong?

What if a user interprets the metric differently?

What if the data changes?

Thinking about failure does not make the work negative.

It makes the solution more trustworthy.

---

## Is This Making Work Easier?

This is one of my favorite questions.

Technology should reduce friction.

A dashboard should not create another task.

An automated workflow should not make exceptions impossible to handle.

A process improvement should not transfer the burden to someone else.

The best solutions make work feel lighter.

---

## Why?

And then I ask why again.

Not to challenge the person.

To understand the problem.

The first answer is often the request.

The second answer reveals the process.

The third answer begins to reveal the real need.

---

## The Question Behind All the Others

At the end, I usually return to one question:

> **Will this help someone make a better decision?**

If the answer is yes, I know what I am building toward.

If the answer is unclear, I keep asking.

---

> **Better questions lead to better insights. Better insights lead to better decisions.**
